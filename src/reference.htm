About Kalman Filter

The Kalman filter is used to improve tracking accuracy by combining predictions with measurements. It helps reduce noise and provides smoother motion estimation.

  • Process Noise: Controls how much we trust our motion model (higher = more responsive to changes)
  • Measurement Noise: Controls how much we trust our sensor measurements (higher = smoother but laggier)
  • Smoothing Factor: Additional smoothing applied to the Kalman output (higher = smoother)

Understanding Kinematic Equations

Kinematic equations are mathematical relationships that describe the motion of objects moving with constant acceleration. They are derived from calculus but can be used algebraically to solve motion problems.

Equation When to Use Missing Variable
v = v₀ + a·t When you don't need displacement Δx
Δx = ½(v₀ + v)·t When you don't have acceleration a
Δx = v₀·t + ½a·t² When final velocity is unknown v
v² = v₀² + 2a·Δx When time is not given t

Key Concepts

  • Acceleration is constant: These equations only work when acceleration doesn't change during the motion.
  • Direction matters: Use positive/negative signs consistently for direction (typically, up/right is positive).
  • Free fall: Near Earth's surface, all objects accelerate downward at g ≈ 9.8 m/s² (ignoring air resistance).
  • Projectile motion: Can be analyzed by separating horizontal and vertical components.
  • Projectile range: Maximum horizontal distance occurs at 45° launch angle.

Common Scenarios

Free Fall: a = -9.8 m/s² (downward)
v = v₀ - 9.8t
Projectile Motion:
Horizontal: a = 0 (constant vₓ)
Vertical: a = -9.8 m/s²
vₓ = v₀·cosθ
vᵧ = v₀·sinθ - 9.8t
Inclined Plane:
Acceleration depends on angle θ
a = g·sinθ

Variable Definitions

v₀ Initial velocity at time t=0 (m/s)
v Final velocity at time t (m/s)
a Constant acceleration (m/s²)
t Elapsed time (seconds)
Δx Displacement (change in position, meters)
θ Launch angle for projectiles (degrees)
